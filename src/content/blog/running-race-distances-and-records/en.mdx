Every runner's journey is a ladder of distances. You start with one that feels possible, train, race it, and the next rung suddenly looks within reach. Here's the full ladder — what each distance is, who it suits, and the world records that show just how far human legs can go.

## The standard distances

### 5K — 5 kilometres
The perfect entry point. Trainable in 6–8 weeks from scratch, short enough to run hard, long enough to be proud of. If you've never raced, start here. (See our guide to [a good 5K time](/blog/what-is-a-good-5k-time).)

### 10K — 10 kilometres
The natural next step and the **most popular race distance in Algeria**. It rewards a bit of endurance without demanding the huge training blocks of longer races — the sweet spot for most recreational runners.

### Half marathon — 21.1 kilometres
The first "big" challenge. It needs a real training block (2–3 months) and teaches you about fuelling and pacing. Finishing your first half marathon is a genuine milestone.

### Marathon — 42.195 kilometres
The classic bucket-list distance. A marathon demands months of dedicated training and respect for the distance — but crossing that finish line is one of the most satisfying things in the sport.

### Ultramarathon — beyond 42.195 km
Anything longer than a marathon: 50K, 100K, and mountain trail ultras. Algeria's *djebels* and deserts are a natural home for these — a growing frontier for adventurous runners.

<Callout type="tip" title="How to climb the ladder">
Don't skip rungs. Run a few 5Ks, move to 10K, then a half, then — if the bug bites — a marathon. Each distance builds the base for the next, and injuries usually come from rushing.
</Callout>

## The world records

To put it all in perspective, here's roughly how fast the very fastest humans cover each distance on the road:

| Distance | Men | Women |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5K | ~12:49 | ~14:13 |
| 10K | ~26:24 | ~28:46 |
| Half marathon | ~57:30 | ~1:02:52 |
| Marathon | 2:00:35 | 2:09:56 |

It's a reminder of how extraordinary the top of the sport is, and how much room every one of us has to simply enjoy the climb.
