Most runners have one speed: as fast as they can manage today. It feels productive, but it's the main reason people plateau, stay tired, and pick up injuries. The fix is almost too simple — **run most of your runs slower** — and heart-rate zones are how you know what "slower" actually means.

## The five zones

Zones are a percentage of your maximum heart rate. A rough estimate of your max is **220 minus your age** (imperfect, but fine to start). From there:

| Zone | % of max HR | Feels like | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Zone 1 | 50–60% | Very easy walk/jog | Warm-up, recovery |
| **Zone 2** | **60–70%** | **Easy, can chat** | **Aerobic base — the big one** |
| Zone 3 | 70–80% | "Comfortably hard" | Steady/tempo |
| Zone 4 | 80–90% | Hard, short sentences | Threshold, speed |
| Zone 5 | 90–100% | All-out | Sprints, VO₂ max |

<Callout type="tip" title="No heart-rate monitor? Use the talk test">
Zone 2 is the pace at which you can hold a full conversation in complete sentences. If you're gasping between words, you've drifted into Zone 3 — slow down.
</Callout>

![Most of your kilometres should feel this relaxed — easy, aerobic, Zone 2.](/blog/heart-rate-zones-and-zone-2/inline-1.jpg)

## Why Zone 2 is the secret

Zone 2 feels *too* easy, which is exactly why most runners skip it. But easy aerobic running is what builds the engine:

- It grows the tiny blood vessels and mitochondria that let your muscles use oxygen efficiently.
- It teaches your body to burn fat for fuel, sparing your limited carbohydrate stores.
- It builds fitness with very little injury or fatigue cost, so you can run more often.

The result: over a few months, you run the **same easy pace at a lower heart rate** — you've literally gotten fitter. Then, when you do run hard, you have a bigger base to push from.

## The 80/20 rule

Elite runners spend roughly **80% of their training easy** (Zone 1–2) and only 20% hard (Zone 3–5). Recreational runners tend to do the opposite — everything in a tiring "medium" — and wonder why they never improve.

<Callout type="warning" title="The most common mistake">
Running your easy days too fast. If every run leaves you drained, you're training your body to be mediocre at everything. Keep easy days genuinely easy so your hard days can be genuinely hard.
</Callout>

## How to start this week

1. Pick your **easy runs** and deliberately slow down until you can chat comfortably. It will feel strange — trust it.
2. Keep **one or two harder sessions** a week (intervals, a tempo run, or hills).
3. Give it 6–8 weeks. Watch your easy-pace heart rate drop.

Zone 2 isn't glamorous, but it's how ordinary runners quietly get fast. Run slow to race fast.
