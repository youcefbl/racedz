"Do I need special shoes for the trail?" It's one of the most common questions runners ask once they venture off the pavement. The honest answer: it depends where and how often you run off-road. Here's exactly what separates the two, so you can decide.

## The four real differences

**1. The outsole (grip).** This is the biggest one. Road shoes have a fairly smooth, flat sole optimised for grip on hard, even surfaces. Trail shoes have deep **lugs** — rubber teeth that bite into dirt, mud, gravel, and rock.

**2. Protection.** Trail shoes add a tougher upper, a reinforced toe bumper, and often a **rock plate** in the sole to stop sharp stones bruising your foot. Road shoes skip all that to stay light.

**3. Stability.** Trail shoes sit lower and wider for a planted, stable feel on uneven ground. Road shoes prioritise a smooth, efficient forward roll.

**4. Cushioning.** Road shoes tend to have more, softer cushioning to absorb the repetitive pounding of tarmac. Trail shoes trade some of that for ground feel and control.

## Side by side

| | Road shoes | Trail shoes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Best surface | Asphalt, concrete, track | Dirt, rock, mud, forest |
| Outsole | Smooth, flat | Deep lugs |
| Protection | Minimal | Rock plate, toe bumper |
| Feel | Cushioned, rolling | Stable, grippy |
| On the wrong surface | Slips on mud | Lugs wear fast, feel clunky on tarmac |

## Which should you buy first?

<Callout type="tip" title="A simple rule">
If **80%+ of your running is on roads and pavements** — like most city runners in Algiers, Oran, or Constantine — buy a **road shoe** first. Only add trail shoes when you're regularly hitting dirt.
</Callout>

Can you mix them? A little. Road shoes handle a smooth gravel path fine. Trail shoes technically work on road, but the lugs wear down quickly on tarmac and feel stiff. For pure road running, road shoes are simply more comfortable and efficient.
