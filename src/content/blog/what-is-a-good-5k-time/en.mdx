The 5K (5 kilometres) is the perfect first race: short enough that anyone can train for it in a couple of months, long enough to feel like a real achievement. The most common question new runners ask is simply — **what's a good time?** Here's an honest answer.

<Callout type="tip" title="The short version">
For a beginner, **finishing** is the goal — that's usually **30–40 minutes**. Anything under **30 minutes** is a solid recreational time. Under **25 minutes** (men) or **28 minutes** (women) is genuinely fit.
</Callout>

## Average 5K times by level

These are broad, real-world ranges for healthy adults. Age, fitness, and terrain all shift them — treat them as landmarks, not verdicts.

| Level | Men | Women |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Beginner (first months) | 32–40 min | 36–44 min |
| Recreational | 25–32 min | 28–36 min |
| Fit amateur | 22–25 min | 25–28 min |
| Advanced | 19–22 min | 22–25 min |
| Competitive | under 19 min | under 22 min |

For reference, the very best runners on earth cover 5K in roughly **13 minutes** — a pace most of us can't hold for even 400 metres. Comparison with elites is fun, but useless for setting your own goals.

Treat this as a 2026 snapshot, not the final word; road records fall often and some are under review. For scale: Kiptum's 2:00:35 marathon is about **2:51 per kilometre** held for 42 km — a pace most of us couldn't sustain for a single lap of a track.

## What actually affects your time

- **Age** — paces naturally slow over the decades; a "good" time at 50 is different from at 25.
- **Training history** — months of consistent running beats any single hard effort.
- **Weight** — carrying less makes every kilometre easier.
- **Course and heat** — a flat, cool morning is far faster than a hilly Algerian afternoon.

## How to run your first 5K faster

1. **Run three times a week.** Consistency beats intensity every time.
2. **Most runs easy** (see our guide to Zone 2), with one faster session weekly.
3. **Add a weekly long run**, slowly building from 3 km toward 6–7 km. Racing 5K feels easy when you routinely run further.
4. **Don't start too fast.** The classic mistake: sprinting the first kilometre, then surviving the rest. Start controlled, finish strong.

<Callout type="warning" title="The only time that matters">
Comparing yourself to others is a fast track to discouragement. The number worth chasing is **your own last time**. Beat that, and you're winning.
</Callout>

Whatever your starting point, a structured couple of months will surprise you. Pick a 5K, put it on the calendar, and give yourself something to train for.
